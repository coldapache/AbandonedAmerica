# Why This Matters

Every abandoned property in America is unrealized potential.

---

## The Problem

Across the United States, millions of properties sit abandoned, condemned, blighted, or chronically vacant. They are the rotting food in the national refrigerator — decay that we walk past every day without a strategy to address it.

Each one of these properties represents:

- **A business that could exist** but doesn't. A storefront, a restaurant, a workshop, an office — sitting empty while entrepreneurs struggle to find affordable space.
- **A home someone could live in** but can't. A house, an apartment, a duplex — deteriorating while people search for affordable housing.
- **Economic value that is evaporating.** Every year a property sits vacant, it loses value. It drags down neighboring property values. It costs the city money in maintenance, code enforcement, and emergency services. It generates zero tax revenue.
- **A community getting weaker**, not stronger. Abandoned buildings attract crime, illegal dumping, and fires. They signal disinvestment. They tell residents and visitors that nobody cares.

## The Scale

We don't fully know how bad it is — and that's part of the problem. There is no national database of abandoned properties. No one has mapped the full scope. Estimates suggest there are **hundreds of billions of dollars** in assessed property value sitting locked up in abandoned, blighted, and vacant buildings across America. That is capital that could be unlocked for entrepreneurs, homeowners, and communities.

This project exists to find out the real number.

## Why It Stays This Way

The system is stacked against fixing this:

- **Overpricing.** Owners of abandoned properties often hold them at unrealistic prices, waiting for a windfall that never comes. Meanwhile the building rots.
- **Zoning and permitting barriers.** Local governments make it expensive and slow to renovate, repurpose, or demolish blighted buildings. Rezoning takes years. Permits stack up.
- **Tax structures that don't penalize vacancy.** In most places, there is no meaningful cost to letting a property sit empty indefinitely. Property taxes on abandoned buildings are often minimal because the assessed value has cratered.
- **Absentee and unresponsive owners.** Many abandoned properties are owned by LLCs, trusts, or out-of-state investors who have no connection to the community and no urgency to act.
- **No centralized data.** Without a clear picture of the problem, there's no pressure to solve it. Cities don't know what other cities are doing. There's no national strategy, no scorecard, no accountability.

## What Could Change

Imagine a world where:

- **Vacancy is disincentivized.** Progressive vacancy taxes make it expensive to let a property sit empty for years. The longer it sits, the more it costs.
- **Sale at fair market value is incentivized.** Tax breaks or credits for owners who sell abandoned properties at realistic prices to buyers who will actually use them.
- **Renovation is encouraged, not blocked.** Streamlined permitting for bringing abandoned properties back to productive use. Fast-track rezoning for blighted areas.
- **Demolition is funded.** When a building is beyond saving, public funding covers demolition so the land can be reused instead of sitting as a hazard.
- **The data is public.** A national database — this project — that makes the scale visible. Policymakers, journalists, researchers, and community organizers can see exactly how many properties are wasted in their city, their state, their country.

## The Macro Punch Line

When we finish mapping this, we'll have a number. A real, verified, dollar amount of assessed property value sitting locked in abandoned buildings across America. Based on what we're finding in individual cities, the national total is likely **hundreds of millions to billions of dollars** in unrealized economic potential.

That is money that could fund:
- Small businesses that create jobs
- Affordable housing that shelters families
- Renovations that revitalize neighborhoods
- Tax revenue that funds schools, roads, and services

Nobody has done this before. There is no national strategy. There is no pathway. There is no incentive structure. There is no centralized effort to even understand the scope of the problem.

This project is the first step: **make it visible.** You can't fix what you can't see.
